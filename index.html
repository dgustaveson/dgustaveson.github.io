A Plain Language Framework

The Sovereignty Reset

You can hold it. You just cannot use it without paying your share.

The federal government spends about $7.4 trillion a year and collects about $5.6 trillion in taxes. Every year the difference gets borrowed. The national debt is now $38.98 trillion. The interest alone costs over a trillion dollars a year — money that leaves the country every single day and helps nobody living in it.

The bottom half of Americans hold about 2.5% of the total wealth in this country. The top 1% hold about 38%. That gap has been growing for four decades and it is not slowing down.

The person who gets up every morning and goes to work pays taxes on every paycheck before they ever see the money. The person who lives off the growth of wealth they already own pays taxes only when they choose to access it. Many never do. They borrow against it instead. That strategy even has a name on Wall Street: Buy Borrow Die.

Both parties have had decades to fix this. Neither has. This is a different starting point.

01
The Scaled Invoice

The American system built the conditions for wealth to grow. Courts. Patents. Roads. The military. An educated public. A stable currency. None of that is free. All of it was paid for collectively.

The more wealth grew inside that system, the larger the fee owed to that system when that growth gets used. Not a punishment. A proportional fee for services received at scale. Same principle for everyone. Scaled to how much the system delivered.

02
The Unified Rule

Value becomes taxable when it becomes usable. A paycheck is usable the moment it arrives. Wealth growth becomes usable when it gets accessed — sold, borrowed against, cashed out, distributed.

Right now the system applies different rules to different kinds of money. A paycheck gets taxed immediately. A billion dollars in stock growth can sit untaxed for decades. The work gets taxed. The winning does not. The unified rule ends that distinction.

03
Building Is Protected

Growing a business. Saving for retirement. Expanding a farm. Reinvesting profits to hire more people. None of that triggers the fee. The fee comes due when growth gets converted into spending power — not when it gets built.

The developer who buys land and sits on it waiting for surrounding neighborhoods to gentrify while contributing nothing pays the fee on the growth every single year. Building is protected. Parking is not.

04
Every Dollar Used Eventually Settles Its Share

Waiting is allowed. Disappearing is not. The liability accrues with interest so waiting is never a free ride. Every year of deferral adds to what is owed. The clock runs. The bill grows. Eventually it gets paid.

No estate planning trick makes it disappear. No offshore structure makes it unreachable. The liability follows the wealth until it is settled.

05
One Rule for Everyone

Right now there are effectively two tax systems running simultaneously. One taxes people whose money comes from working. The other applies to people whose money comes from owning things.

Warren Buffett has said publicly that he pays a lower rate than his secretary. That is not an anomaly. That is the system functioning as designed. One rule for everyone means the source of the money stops mattering.

06
A Framework That Can Pay the Bills Without More Borrowing

Three taxes replace the current income tax system. A threshold income tax of 10% on all gross income above roughly $45,000 — no deductions, no loopholes. An entitlement tax that restructures Social Security and Medicare contributions. A Wealth Growth Excise — a continuous progressive fee on annual wealth growth above $25,000, rising to 91% on growth above $1 billion annually.

That rate is not new. It existed during the Eisenhower administration when America built its middle class, constructed the interstate highway system, and paid its post-war debts simultaneously.

The deficit stops on day one. Not gradually. Not eventually.

07
More Left in the Pocket

Federal income tax on wages phases out as the framework matures. The paycheck gets bigger without the employer changing anything. Social Security gets funded with real money. Housing becomes more affordable when idle land becomes expensive to hoard. Inflation slows when the government stops borrowing to cover deficits.

And something harder to measure: when working people keep more of what they earn, some of them build things. Not all. But some. That is how innovation actually works at the ground level. Not in boardrooms. In garages. On kitchen tables at midnight. With a little more money left at the end of the month than there used to be.

You grew wealthy using America. This is America's invoice.
The more the system delivered, the larger the fee when those gains get used.

This framework is a starting point, not a finished law. The math requires economists. The law requires lawyers. The politics requires people who show up.
